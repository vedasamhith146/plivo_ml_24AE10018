Language Model Training Summary

This report summarizes the work recorded in NOTES.md, RUNLOG.md, and the implementation in model.py and train.py. No separate nodes.md file was present in the workspace, so the summary below uses the available experiment notes and the actual architecture used for training.

Objective

The goal was to train a small GPT-style language model under the assignment constraints: CPU-only training, at most 2,000 optimizer steps, at most 2,000,000 parameters, and using only the provided training corpus. The primary evaluation metric was dev bits-per-byte (bpb), where lower is better.

What Changed Across the Experiments

  • Early experiments tested weight tying, deeper layers, new initialization, and alternative optimizers. Most of these did not help enough or made the model worse.
  • Embedding width was the first change that clearly improved performance, showing that model capacity mattered more than simply adding depth.
  • Training stability improved with gradient clipping and a larger batch size, which made updates less noisy.
  • Learning-rate behavior was refined with a warmup-and-decay schedule, and the final setup used a stronger optimizer configuration that worked better once the rest of the setup had been improved.

Best Result

The strongest run achieved a dev bpb of 1.9704. This is substantially better than the baseline score of 2.3718.

Why this is the best result:

  • It produced the lowest validation bpb among the tested runs.
  • It stayed within the required capacity and step constraints.
  • It combined better model capacity, a stable optimization setup, and more reliable training dynamics.
  • It suggests that the most important gains came from width, stability, and optimizer tuning rather than from simply making the network deeper.

Why This Setup Is the Strongest

The final architecture and training recipe performed well because it balanced capacity with stability. The wider embedding dimension gave the model more representational space, while gradient clipping and a larger batch size reduced unstable updates. The final optimizer choice and learning-rate schedule helped the model use its limited training budget more effectively.

In short, the best run was not just a bigger model; it was a better-trained model.

Machine Work vs. Human Work

Roughly, the project was about 85% machine-done and 15% human-done.

  • Machine-done: training runs, evaluation, metric logging, checkpoint saving, repeated optimization steps, and numerical comparison across runs.
  • Human-done: selecting hypotheses, deciding which changes to try, interpreting the results, and choosing the final configuration based on the evidence.

This ratio is reasonable because the actual learning and scoring were automated by the training pipeline, while the overall strategy and final judgment were guided by human reasoning.

Final Conclusion

The best-performing model is the one that achieved 1.9704 bpb through a combination of stronger representational capacity and a more stable training recipe. The main lesson from the experiments is that, in this constrained setting, improving width and optimization quality mattered more than simply increasing depth.
